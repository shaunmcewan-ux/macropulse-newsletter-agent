Chart Spotlight · 01
SP500 — this week

The S&P 500 closed at 7,412.66 on Friday, up 0.25% on the day but still down 2.46% for the week — a recovery that masks a sharp round trip. Wednesday's session marked the index's lowest close in a month after a disappointing Oracle earnings report combined with hawkish Iran rhetoric out of Washington to trigger a broad AI-linked sell-off. The chart shows that washout testing the rising moving-average support that has held since the spring advance, before Thursday's 1.75% rebound (Nasdaq +2.54%, Dow +1.86%) recovered most of the lost ground.

The rebound's catalyst was a de-escalation in US-Iran tensions alongside the pricing of SpaceX's IPO after Thursday's close — at $135 a share, the offering raised $75B and valued the company at $1.77T, the largest IPO in history. SpaceX itself is not yet S&P 500-eligible (it needs 12 months of trading first), but near-term mechanical buying from Nasdaq-100 and Russell index funds — estimated at $22B-$27B — was enough to pull risk appetite back into mega-cap tech and semiconductors into Friday.

For the dial, the read-through is limited but consistent with this week's theme: a shock (hot CPI and PPI, Middle East tensions) that should have been unambiguously risk-off instead resolved with equities back near range highs, the dollar below 100, and BTC up on the week. The index remains inside its multi-month uptrend; a clean reclaim of Wednesday's pre-selloff level would confirm the dip as noise rather than a trend change heading into next week's FOMC.

Chart Spotlight · 02
BTCTREASURY — this week

This week's other structural story sits away from the macro calendar: corporate Bitcoin treasuries, and a widening split in how they're behaving. Strategy (formerly MicroStrategy) now holds 845,256 BTC — by a wide margin the largest of any public company, and roughly three-quarters of all corporate-held Bitcoin — after adding 1,550 BTC for $101.3M (average $65,332) in the week of June 1-7, funded through at-the-market sales of MSTR shares. The purchase came days after the company's first confirmed BTC sale in years, a token 35-coin tax-related disposal on June 1 — Strategy sold, then kept buying.

The chart shows the scale gap on a log axis: Strategy's holdings dwarf the rest of the field. Metaplanet is the other net accumulator, having added 5,075 BTC in Q1 to reach 40,177 — enough to overtake Riot Platforms in the global rankings. By contrast, the two largest US miners have been trimming: Marathon (38,689 BTC) and Riot Platforms (15,680 BTC, down from 19,223 a year ago after selling 3,778 BTC in Q1) are both net sellers, monetising mined supply rather than holding it.

The aggregate picture — roughly 154 public companies hold about 5.35% of Bitcoin's 21M supply, worth near $80.5B — is supportive on its face, but the composition has narrowed sharply: Strategy now accounts for the large majority of net corporate buying, while non-Strategy accumulators added roughly 1,000 BTC combined over the past 30 days against Strategy's tens of thousands. That concentration doesn't change this week's BTC dominance read (firm near 56.4%, no Alt Rotation signal), but it is a single point of dependency worth monitoring — continued accumulation from one buyer is a thinner support than broad-based corporate demand.
