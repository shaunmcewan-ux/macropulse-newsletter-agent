Chart Spotlight · 01
BTC.D — the cycle tell

BTC dominance is the cleanest tell in the framework right now, and this week it did nothing — which is precisely the point. BTC.D sits at 55.5%, holding the same 55–56% band it has occupied for weeks, with BTC itself range-bound near $61,800 and funding flat near zero. Structurally the chart is a consolidation: a firm floor around 55% and no impulsive move in either direction, even as TOTAL recovered to $2.21T and the alt complex tried to catch a bid.

The level that matters is the sub-50% line — the Alt Rotation trigger — and it remains far away. As long as dominance holds above its rising support near 55%, capital stays concentrated in Bitcoin rather than broadening into alts, and the dial reads accumulation, not rotation. Momentum is neutral: no breakdown, no breakout, the 55% pivot acting as the axis.

For the dial, this is why a recovering total market cap did not tip the read toward Alt Rotation. Breadth has not broadened; the recovery in TOTAL was led by BTC, not the alt majors, and BTC.D's firmness confirms it. A sustained break below ~53% would be the first evidence of genuine rotation; until then, the cycle tell says the same thing the scorecard does — BTC Accumulation, holding.

Chart Spotlight · 02
10Y−2Y curve — testing the lower trendline

The 10Y–2Y curve is the chart that explains why the dial's real-yield headwind survived a dovish labour week. The spread sits at +0.31%, flattened from roughly +0.40% in early June, and the flattening this week was the telling part: even into a payrolls print that missed by half, the long end held firm (10Y at 4.48%) while the 2Y stayed sticky near 4.17%. That is a bear-flattening — the market refusing to price aggressive cuts at the long end despite the soft data.

Structurally the curve is positively sloped and well clear of the −50bps deep-inversion threshold that would flip it to a headwind, so the indicator itself stays neutral. But the direction is the signal: a flattening long end with sticky term premium is exactly what keeps DFII10 real yields pinned at 2.26%, and it is real yields — not the payrolls line — that the dial scores as a headwind.

The implication runs straight into next week. If June CPI on July 14 prints soft, the front end can rally on revived cut expectations and bull-steepen the curve, which would finally begin easing the real-yield drag and could lift the dial's conviction off weakening. A hot print does the opposite — it entrenches the long end, holds real yields above 2%, and keeps the dial capped. The curve is the transmission line to watch.
