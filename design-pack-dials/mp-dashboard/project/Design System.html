System Reference

One quiet system, six pages to align.

The Dashboard set the canon. This document extracts that canon into reusable tokens and patterns, then walks each public page to flag the divergences and prescribe the smallest fix that brings it back into the family.

For: site-wide rollout Reference: /dashboard/ Voice: calm, research-led, editorial
A note on what's observed vs. inferred. The dashboard tokens below are extracted directly from the shipped design. The homepage audit is grounded in the live page content. The methodology, about, newsletter, contact, FAQ, and legal audits are framed as checks against the system — written as the kinds of divergence likely to need attention on a site that grew page-by-page. Treat each finding as a hypothesis to verify in the live build, not a pixel measurement.

01

Typography

Three families, each with one job. Fraunces for editorial weight, Inter for interface, JetBrains Mono for the small print and the data.

The three families

Fraunces carries voice — every headline, every editorial body paragraph, every cycle-state name. Inter carries interface — buttons, navigation, form fields, inline UI. JetBrains Mono carries metadata and data — eyebrows, datelines, ticker prices, byline rules. The rule: if a reader would read it, it's Fraunces; if they'd act on it, Inter; if they'd glance at it, Mono.

Three rules that keep it calm

One italic per viewport. The orange-gradient italic Fraunces is a signal device — the cycle state, a hero word, an editorial pull. Use it more than once on screen and it stops being a signal.

No font-weight other than 400 or 500. Bold (600+) only inside running prose where genuine emphasis is needed. Do not weight-bomb headlines.

Mono is metadata, never body. If a paragraph is set in mono, you have a label, not prose. Rewrite as prose or mark it as a label and shorten.

The orange rule

Orange appears once per viewport as the live-signal colour. On /dashboard/ that's the active cycle dot. On marketing pages it's the eyebrow dot or a single CTA — never both at once. Decorative orange (gradient borders on every card, orange section backgrounds, orange dividers) is the single biggest tell that a page has drifted from the system.

Green and red never appear in marketing copy. They are data colours: arrows, deltas, scorecard rules. If you find yourself reaching for green to say "good," use ink instead and let the writing do the work.

Two rules

Page rhythm is 96/56/24. 96px between top-level sections, 56px between sub-sections inside a section, 24px between blocks inside a sub-section. Memorise that triple — it produces the whole site's vertical cadence.

Page width is 1180px. Side padding is 40px desktop, 20px mobile. Editorial measure inside that container should not exceed ~62–66ch for body copy.

Seven patterns that compose almost every screen. Specs are CSS-ready — paste, then tune.

Eyebrow label

The kicker above every section heading. Always mono, always uppercase, always 11px / 0.16em tracking. Two variants: plain (just the text, used inline above headings) and pill (with a glowing orange dot, used at page-level above the doc title).

Homepage

72Conformance

Voice and structure are right — cycle-first thinking, clear regime framing, four-state Cycle Indicator anchored as the page's spine. The gaps are mostly about density of patterns: too many CTAs, too many supporting headers without eyebrows, and a hero that asks the visitor to read four sentences before they meet the cycle states.

  1. 01
    Hero buries the regime call

    "Track the Cycle, Seize the Trend — Turning Macro Insight into Market Clarity" is two slogans glued together, then a 60-word paragraph, then two CTAs. The four cycle states arrive after a scroll.

    Cut the hero to one display headline, one 22px lede, the four cycle-state chips inline below it, and one primary CTA. Move the second CTA to the end-of-page CTA strip. The Dashboard's "thirty-second answer" rule applies to the homepage too.

  2. 02
    The four "weekly / 4-state / direct / allocation" tiles repeat what's just been said

    Below the hero, four bold-prefixed paragraphs summarise the same value props the hero just made. This is content padding, not a design element.

    Delete or replace with a single horizontal "what you get" rule: four mono-eyebrow labels separated by hairline ticks (Weekly · 4 states · Direct · Allocation) above the next section. Frees ~400px of vertical space.

  3. 03
    Section heads are missing eyebrows

    "What MacroPulse Delivers", "Methodology Snapshot", "Sample Research", "Newsletter Archive", "Subscribe / Join Waitlist" sit as inline H2 strings without the mono kicker pattern that defines the section-header component.

    Promote each to the canonical pattern: mono orange-2 eyebrow ("METHODOLOGY · SNAPSHOT") on top, Fraunces H2 below, italic 16px lede beneath. Establishes site-wide rhythm in one pass.

  4. 04
    Three sample-issue cards lack a card chassis

    "Sample Issue / March 2026", "Historical Lens / February 2026", "Framework Note / January 2026" read as three stacked headlines with bodies, not as a card grid. Loss of visual rhythm.

    Wrap each in the Card component (see §04). Use the dateline pattern: mono kicker line "SAMPLE ISSUE · MAR 2026", then headline, then body, then a ghost link "Read the sample format →". Three-column grid on desktop, stacked on mobile.

  5. 05
    Subscribe block embeds a Beehiiv URL as a link

    An exposed subscribe-forms.beehiiv.com URL appears as link text. Reads as a developer leftover; breaks the editorial frame.

    Replace with the Form panel component — orange-2 "SUBSCRIBE" label, "Start with the newsletter." headline, email input + primary CTA, "Free · No spam · Unsubscribe anytime" mono foot. Iframe the Beehiiv form behind that chrome.

  6. 06
    Trust-line trio at the bottom has no container

    "Research & education only · UK/US-aware legal framing · No hard performance claims" floats as three orphan phrases.

    Set as a single mono row, 11px ink-3, separated by " · " glyphs, centred above the footer rule. Standard footer-disclosure pattern.

/methodology/

Methodology

?Likely 60–70

Of all six non-dashboard pages, this one most wants to feel like an editorial document. Methodology is where MacroPulse explains its mind — it should read like a research paper, not a marketing page.

  1. 01
    Likely missing a documented type rhythm

    Long technical pages tend to drift to a single body size with bold subheads — flat, hard to scan.

    Adopt a strict three-tier rhythm: H1 (44px Fraunces) per chapter, H2 (28px) per indicator, H3 (19px italic) per sub-aspect. Body is 17px Fraunces with 62ch measure. One eyebrow per chapter, never per paragraph.

  2. 02
    Indicator list probably reads as a flat bullet list

    "Global liquidity / Rates / PMI / USD strength / Fed balance sheet" works as a teaser on the homepage but a methodology page should expand each.

    Convert to a numbered indicator essay pattern: 01 — Global liquidity as Fraunces italic display number + headline, body paragraph, and a small mono "what we watch / why it matters / how it scores" tri-line at the foot. Repeat for all five.

  3. 03
    Probably no visible TOC

    A long-form research page without a contents block forces readers to scroll-hunt.

    Add the TOC component used at the top of this document — bordered card, mono "CONTENTS" label, two-column ordered list with decimal-leading-zero numerals in orange-2, anchored links.

  4. 04
    No printable / shareable affordance

    Methodology is the page allocators screenshot for compliance files — it should know that.

    Add a small mono "Last revised · 8 May 2026" line under the H1 and a print stylesheet that strips backgrounds, sets ink to #101820, and keeps the H/body rhythm. No other "share" UI needed.

/about/

About

?Likely 65–75

About pages on indie research sites tend to drift toward founder-portrait plus paragraph — fine, but it should still read like the rest of MacroPulse.

  1. 01
    "About us" section heading is generic

    Likely uses a literal "About" H1 with a body block beneath.

    Replace with an editorial title in the system display style — e.g. "Built by a single voice, for serious readers." — mono eyebrow "ABOUT" above. Same component as homepage section heads.

  2. 02
    Author bio probably lacks structure

    Single block of paragraphs with no datelines or markers.

    Set as a two-column grid: left column 280px portrait slot (or initials-only block in bg-3 if no photo), right column with name as Fraunces 28px, role as italic 19px, bio as 17px serif body. Below: a "Beliefs" tri-card row reusing the Card component — three short statements that anchor the editorial point of view.

  3. 03
    End-of-page action probably absent or duplicates homepage

    If there's no CTA, the page leaks. If it's a copy of the homepage block, it feels like padding.

    Use the CTA strip with a contextual line — "If this is the lens you've been missing, start with the newsletter." — single primary button to /newsletter/.

/newsletter/

Newsletter

?Likely 55–70

This is the conversion page; it tends to attract "marketing pattern" gravity (testimonial sliders, hero-with-screenshot, social proof counters) that fights the editorial voice.

  1. 01
    Embedded Beehiiv form probably renders raw

    Default Beehiiv styling collides with the dark editorial palette — wrong fonts, wrong button radius, wrong primary colour.

    Wrap the Beehiiv iframe in the Form panel chassis. If their styles override yours, override back at the host: white-on-dark inputs, primary button in orange gradient, mono "free · no spam · unsubscribe anytime" foot. Treat the iframe as a black box and frame it.

  2. 02
    "Sample issues" likely repeat homepage tiles

    If the homepage already shows three sample cards, the newsletter page repeating the same three is redundant.

    Make this page the archive index. List 8–12 issues as a single dateline table: mono date, Fraunces 19px headline, italic 14px regime tag (e.g. "BTC Accumulation"). Hairline rule between rows. Reuse the dataset, not the homepage components.

  3. 03
    "What you get" probably needs to be more specific than the homepage

    Homepage's value props are generic ("concise briefings"); the newsletter page is where to be concrete.

    Add a 3-column grid below the form: each column = one mono eyebrow + Fraunces 22px headline + 14.5px body. E.g. "Cadence — Every Tuesday, 7am UK." / "Length — ~1,400 words. ~6 minutes." / "Anchor — One regime call per issue, with rationale." Be exact; allocators reward exactness.

/contact/

Contact

?Likely 60–75

Contact pages on small sites tend to be either "just an email link" or "a 12-field form" — neither fits a calm editorial brand.

  1. 01
    Probably presents email as info@macropulse.uk link only

    Functional but cold; misses an editorial moment.

    One-screen layout: left column has a 22px italic Fraunces lede ("Press, partnerships, or a question about the methodology — write directly."), then three labelled lines (Email · Response time · Best for). Right column has the Form panel for shorter notes. Single page, single screen on desktop.

  2. 02
    Form probably lacks the "best for" routing

    A generic name/email/message form doesn't tell the visitor what kind of message belongs here.

    Add a small mono "Reason" radio/select with three options — Press, Subscriber question, Partnership — above the message field. Routes the email and shows the reader you've thought about it.

/faq/

FAQ

?Likely 50–65

FAQ pages are where systems break — the accordion pattern is so conventional it usually gets dropped in raw, with no type or colour treatment.

  1. 01
    Likely uses a default <details> accordion with no styling

    Native disclosure widget reads as untreated; the disclosure triangle doesn't match the system's mono/orange language.

    Style <details>: 1px line bottom border per item, no top/side borders. <summary> set in Fraunces 19px ink, with a custom "+" / "−" glyph in mono orange-2 on the right edge that rotates on open. Body in 17px Fraunces ink-2 with 14px top padding. No card chassis around the whole accordion — let it be a bare list.

  2. 02
    Probably no category structure

    Single flat list of all questions makes the page hard to skim.

    Group into three sections: The newsletter, The cycle indicator, Subscriptions & access. Each gets the section-header component (mono eyebrow, H2). The accordion sits within each.

  3. 03
    End-of-page probably leaves the reader stranded

    Last accordion → footer is a rough exit.

    Footer-adjacent: a quiet two-line block — "Didn't find what you needed? Write directly." — with a ghost button to /contact/. Not a CTA strip; this is a softer hand-off.

/legal/

Legal

?Likely 70–85

Legal usually conforms by accident — boring HTML, default sans, a wall of paragraphs. The fix is small: make it look intentional.

  1. 01
    Likely set in Inter at body size, not Fraunces

    Legal text in sans reads like a developer dumped the template; out of family with the rest of the site.

    Set body as Fraunces 16px / 1.65, ink-2. Keep mono only for the "Last revised" dateline and for any code or address blocks. Cap measure at ~62ch even if the page is otherwise full-width.

  2. 02
    Section numbering probably uses default ol

    Browser default counters look like a Word document.

    Use the same counter-increment + ::before pattern as the section headers in this doc — decimal-leading-zero in orange-2 mono, sat in a 28px gutter to the left of each H2. Lifts the page into the family for almost no work.

06

Do not propagate

Six dashboard moves that make /dashboard/ work but would make every other page feel tool-shaped. Keep them where they belong.

The sticky tape rail
Stays on /dashboard/

Twelve live prices in a 260px sticky left rail is right when the page's job is to surface market state. On marketing pages it would scream "trading dashboard" and undermine the calm voice. The footer-adjacent "current sample state · BTC Accumulation" line on the homepage is enough.

The pulse / live indicator dot
Stays on /dashboard/ mast only

The glowing orange "● LIVE" dot is a freshness signal — it implies the page updates. The newsletter, methodology, and about pages don't update on their own; the dot would be a false promise. Use the static eyebrow pill on every other page.

"Refreshed Nm ago" timestamps
Stays on /dashboard/ mast

Same reason. On methodology, use "Last revised · 8 May 2026" instead — calmer, semantically correct.

The four-cycle-state rail
Limited cameo on / and /methodology/

The full active state + 30d-ago ghost indicator is the dashboard's signature. The marketing pages can show a static four-state rail (no live dot, no ghost) as a teaser, but never the live version. One canonical live instance — that's how it stays a signature.

Scorecard status glyphs (▮ ▯ ▭)
Stays on /dashboard/

The compact "supportive / neutral / headwind" glyph language belongs to the data-dense scorecard. On methodology, describe each indicator in prose. Outside the dashboard, status conveyed through symbols without legends reads as gnomic.

Tabular-numerals price lists
Stays on /dashboard/

Mono price grids are utility chrome. On the homepage and newsletter page, mention BTC and ETH in prose — not as a 12-row tape. The newsletter page's value prop isn't "we have prices"; it's "we have a regime call."

The single rule behind the list

The dashboard is a live instrument; everything else is a document. Anything on /dashboard/ that depends on the page being alive — tickers, timestamps, live indicators, sticky tapes — does not belong on a document. Anything that's about voice, type, colour, rhythm, or component chassis travels freely. Use that boundary as the test when in doubt.
