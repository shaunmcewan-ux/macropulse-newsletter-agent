01
Ship nowTrust-buildingSEO upside

Indicator glossary

You reference M2, PMI, real yields, BTC.D and TOTAL like the reader knows them. A glossary turns that confidence into onboarding.

Why it matters now

Your audience is split: half are macro-fluent allocators who don't need definitions; half are crypto-native traders who recognise BTC.D but not real yields, or vice versa. A glossary lets the prose stay tight (no parenthetical definitions interrupting flow) while still being legible to both. Bonus: each indicator page is a clean long-tail SEO target — "what is BTC dominance" gets searched.

What it contains
  • HeaderOne-line definition in Fraunces 22px italic. The kind of sentence you'd want a colleague to memorise.
  • Why we watch itTwo paragraphs of editorial body. How it moves the cycle. Not a textbook.
  • How we score itTier (1/2/3), threshold for supportive/neutral/headwind, source. Mono mini-table.
  • Recent readingLatest value pulled from the dashboard, with the 30-day direction arrow. Live, but quiet.
  • Further readingThree external links — FRED, BIS, a textbook chapter. No affiliate stuff.
Initial set

Twelve indicators total, matching the dashboard scorecard: M2, ISM, DXY, Real Yields, 2s10s, Fed Balance Sheet (Tier 1 & 2); BTC, ETH, ETH/BTC, BTC.D, TOTAL, Funding, Stablecoin Dominance (Tier 3).

Placement

Index page at /methodology/glossary/, linked from the methodology TOC and from the footer's "Reference" column. Each indicator has its own URL — /methodology/glossary/m2/ — and the scorecard rows on /dashboard/ link to them via a small mono "↗ define" affordance.

02
Ship nowConversion-critical

Sample-issues archive

The newsletter page should be an archive, not a pitch. Allocators want to read three issues before they pay for the fourth.

Why it matters now

Right now /newsletter/ is a conversion surface. That's the wrong job: before someone pays £19/month they want to see how you actually write, not how you describe how you write. The homepage shows three sample tiles — three is too few. An archive of every issue, free through the launch of the paid tier is the cheapest, highest-trust thing you can build.

What it contains
  • HeaderMono eyebrow "ARCHIVE", Fraunces H1 "Every weekly note, since launch.", italic 19px lede about cadence and length.
  • Row per issueMono date · Fraunces 19px headline · italic 14px regime tag (e.g. "BTC Accumulation"). Hairline rule between. Click opens the issue.
  • Issue pageDate + regime tag + headline + body, set in your editorial body type. Linked to the dashboard state on that date when possible.
  • Search / filterOptional later — for now, chronological is enough. Group by month at most.
  • Footer CTAOne CTA strip at the bottom: "Read it as it lands — subscribe." Not above the archive; archives are not pitches.
Placement

Repurpose /newsletter/ as the archive index. The current marketing copy moves to a smaller "About the newsletter" block above the archive — or merges into /about/. New issues live at /newsletter/2026-05-12-cpi-week/ — date-slug URLs.

03
WaitTied to paid launch

Pricing page

A pricing page before you have something to charge for is a promise you can't keep. Ship it the day the paid tier opens, not before.

Why wait, specifically

Two costs to shipping pricing early. First, an empty paid tier is worse than no paid tier — visitors form the impression "they're trying to charge but no one's buying." Second, you'll iterate the tier definitions twice before launch; pages live in your sitemap accrue SEO weight you don't want pointing at draft pricing.

That said, you should design the page now (so launch day is a flip-the-switch moment) and you should expose a tiny "paid tier coming" line on the homepage as soft validation marketing.

What it contains (when it ships)
  • HeroMono eyebrow "PRICING", Fraunces H1 "Three tiers. The same lens." Italic lede: "Pick the cadence that fits how you read."
  • Three columnsFree · Weekly £19/mo · Pro £79/mo. Each column: name, single-line promise, price, "What you get" 5-item list, single CTA.
  • Pro columnSubtle orange-2 hairline along its top edge — the system's signal accent. No "MOST POPULAR" badge; that's marketing-page language. Use the design system's restraint.
  • Annual toggleMono "Monthly / Annual" switch above the columns. Annual at 10× monthly (i.e. two months free).
  • Below the columnsFAQ-style — "Can I expense this?", "Do you offer team pricing?", "Will free tier still exist?" — five questions max. Reuses the FAQ accordion pattern.
  • Compliance lineMono fine-print at the bottom: "Research & education only. Not investment advice." Links to /legal/disclaimer/.
Placement

New primary nav item at launch: /pricing/. Until then, keep it out of nav entirely — pre-launch hint lives as a single italic line above the footer: "Free now. A paid tier is in beta — join the waitlist."

04
Wait — ~100 paid subsQuality-gated

Testimonials & social proof

Bad testimonials are worse than none. Wait until you have three you'd put on a billboard — then place them, don't pile them.

Why wait, specifically

The wrong testimonial signals "we needed to prove we had readers." The right one signals "this person reads us and you'd respect them." At your stage, MacroPulse's brand is more credible than any quote you could harvest. Premature social proof is dilutive.

Set a trigger: once three subscribers who are attributable (CIO/PM/strategist at a recognisable firm, or a well-followed analyst) say something usable in an email, ship the strip. Until then, don't.

What it contains (when it ships)
  • FormatThree Fraunces 19px italic pull-quotes in a row, each ~30 words. Below each: mono "NAME · ROLE · FIRM" in ink-3. No photos.
  • PlacementOne band on the homepage, between "Methodology Snapshot" and the sample-issues archive. One band on the newsletter page, just above the form. Never both visible together.
  • No subscriber countsDon't post "joined by 1,200 allocators." Counts age badly; a strong quote ages forever.
  • No firm logosLogos read as enterprise software, not editorial. Stick to text attribution.
Placement

Inline only — never a /testimonials/ page. Hidden in code now, behind a feature flag, so you can drop the three quotes in without a new section.

05
Ship nowPositioning-critical

"How we differ" comparison

Visitors arrive holding a mental map: Glassnode = on-chain data, Bankless = crypto-native journalism, CT = noise. Tell them where you sit on that map before they have to guess.

Why it matters now

At your stage every visitor is doing comparison shopping. If you don't position, they position for you — and they'll guess wrong roughly half the time. The cost of writing this section is ninety minutes; the cost of not writing it is every visitor who decides "this is just another crypto newsletter."

What it contains
  • Not a feature tableResist the urge to draw a Glassnode-vs-MacroPulse comparison grid. Feature tables read as combative and date badly. Use prose.
  • Three short paragraphsOne each for the three reference points: on-chain platforms, crypto-native media, retail commentary. Each ends with the contrast sentence.
  • Example contrast linesOn-chain platforms give you the data. We give you the read. · Crypto-native media tells you what happened. We tell you which regime you're in. · Twitter is opinion at retail tempo. MacroPulse is one disciplined call per week.
  • A closing positioning sentence"MacroPulse is the macro-first lens for people who already have data tools and want a thesis."
Placement

On /about/, as the second section after the founder bio. Titled "How this is different" with a mono eyebrow "POSITIONING". Not its own page — that frames it as defensive. As a section it reads as confident.

06
Skip for nowDemand-driven

Press / media kit

A press kit is a response to inbound press, not a substitute for it. Build it when the third journalist asks.

Why skip, specifically

Two reasons. First, the things in a press kit (logo SVG, two-line bio, founder photo, one screenshot) are five minutes of work when asked — not a page to be designed. Second, having a /press/ page when no press has called signals self-importance. The first journalist sending a DM gets a Notion link with the assets. The third gets a /press/ page.

When the time comes, the page is short: bio at three lengths (50 / 150 / 400 words), founder portrait at two crops, logo SVG light/dark, three dashboard screenshots, contact email. Five minutes of layout once you have the assets ready.

Placement

When it ships: footer-only link to /press/. Never in primary nav. For now: keep the assets in a Notion page; share that link when asked.

07
Ship nowPre-paid-tier essentialAdvisory caveat — not legal advice

UK regulatory disclosure

The paid tier is a tripwire. Make sure the disclosure footing is in place before you charge, not after the first complaint.

The shape of the issue

The UK regulator's concern is the line between journalism / commentary (largely unregulated when generic) and investment advice (regulated). Paid newsletters in the UK typically sit on the journalism side provided they:

— make no personal recommendations (i.e. content is for a general audience, not tailored to one reader's circumstances); — make no performance claims framed as predictions of future return; — clearly disclose that content is research and education, not investment advice; — clearly disclose any positions held by the author in assets discussed, or commit to none.

Crypto-specific marketing rules (the FCA's October 2023 financial promotions regime) layer additional constraints on anything that looks like promotion of a crypto asset. A research newsletter that comments on BTC/ETH should be careful never to phrase content as "you should buy" — the constraint is real and the wording matters.

What ships — three placements
  • Dedicated page/legal/disclaimer/ — a long-form research disclaimer. Sections: nature of content, no personal advice, no performance guarantees, conflicts policy (positions held), distribution, jurisdictional notes (UK / US-aware), how to contact for corrections.
  • Footer lineMono 11px on every page: "Research & education only · Not investment advice · Full disclaimer ↗"
  • Issue boilerplateEach newsletter issue ends with a 2-line italic footer: "MacroPulse is research and education, not investment advice. The author may hold positions in assets mentioned."
  • Checkout copyWhen the paid tier ships, the Stripe checkout includes a mandatory checkbox: "I understand MacroPulse is research, not investment advice."
  • About-page lineOne italic sentence in the bio: "MacroPulse is published by [Co. Name] Ltd, registered in England (#XXXXXXXX). It is research and education, not investment advice."
One thing to verify with a solicitor

Specifically ask about the FCA's October 2023 financial promotions regime as it applies to paid crypto-related content distributed by a UK Ltd. — whether commentary that names specific crypto assets (BTC, ETH) needs an FCA-authorised approver for the marketing copy, even if the editorial content does not. The answer drives whether your homepage and pricing-page copy need an approver pathway, separate from your editorial disclaimer.

Placement

Three places: /legal/disclaimer/ as the canonical page; footer mono line site-wide; and a 2-line italic boilerplate in every issue's footer. Get the solicitor sign-off before the paid tier opens.

Ship nowLow costSignals seriousness

RSS + follow without subscribing

Macro readers live in Feedbin. Ship RSS and you'll be read by people who never gave you an email — which is fine, because some of them will email later.

Why it matters now

The audience you want — buy-side macro types, ex-rates traders, family-office allocators — disproportionately uses RSS. Beehiiv emits RSS by default; the only work is exposing it in the footer. A second affordance: a "view in browser" archive (which you'll already have via gap §02). Together they say "you can follow us without giving us your email yet" — a quietly confident move at this stage.

What it contains
  • Footer "Follow" clusterThree mono labels in a row: Email · RSS · X (optional). RSS link goes to the Beehiiv-generated /feed.xml, surfaced through your domain via a redirect.
  • Dashboard RSS (optional)Separate feed for dashboard state changes — one entry per cycle-state transition. Niche but appreciated by the audience.
  • JSON Feed alongsideModern readers prefer JSON Feed. Beehiiv doesn't emit it; trivially convertible via a single static endpoint. Skip if it's friction.
  • OPMLSkip. No one will use it.
Placement

Footer "Follow" cluster only — no primary nav real estate. Surface the URL pattern as /feed.xml on your own domain (not the Beehiiv URL) so you can move providers later without breaking subscribers.
