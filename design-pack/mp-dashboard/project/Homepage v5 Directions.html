Homepage v5 · 3 directions + 5 copy sets
Internal · 11 May 2026
Design Review · Pass 5

Solving for "feels basic" without becoming a marketing page.

The current homepage ships restraint correctly. What it doesn't yet ship is density — the sense, on first scroll, that there's substance here. Three structural directions, five voice sets, one recommendation.

Inputs: v4 live, field feedback Brief: density without noise System: locked

The diagnosis

The cycle headline is doing its job — it lands. The page around it isn't. A first-time reader sees one big phrase and one CTA and infers that's the whole product. The dashboard earns its visit because every scroll yields something to look at; the homepage doesn't return on its scroll. That's "feels basic."

The constraint

Density is the enemy of restraint unless it's evidence. Anything we add must be the same kind of thing the dashboard is — a number, a name, a date, a sentence the reader can verify. No testimonials, no carousel, no hero with gradient blobs. Calm signal-density.

I

Three structural directions

Each takes a different theory of what "less basic" means. Mobile-first wires for 390px, then desktop wires for 1180px. Section change-list, named visual moves, trade-offs, effort, marketing-page risk.

Keep the cycle headline. Surround it with the kind of quiet evidence that lives on the dashboard — five indicator readings, a single inline chart, a "weeks in state" counter. Density via numbers, not via more components.

Visual moves that make it less sparse
Wire-service topline
A five-token mono strip at the very top — date, issue, regime, week. Reads as a newspaper masthead line, not a marketing eyebrow. Costs ~36px vertical, adds five facts.
Evidence panel
A second column on desktop, full-width row on mobile. Five readings in the dashboard's exact typographic style. Makes the homepage feel like a public face of the dashboard, not a separate marketing artefact.
Single inline chart
One chart, ~280px tall, two series. The discipline of "exactly one" is what keeps it editorial. A page with five charts is a dashboard; a page with one chart is a publication.
"Now in its 7th week."
A single-sentence subhead under the cycle word. Implies track record, implies stability of method, implies that this regime has a history — all without claiming anything.
Trade-offs vs. current (v4)

Better

  • Density problem solved with substance: five facts, one chart, one duration claim.
  • The homepage and dashboard share a typographic vocabulary — they read as the same product.
  • "7th week" gives the visitor a sense of time passing without a live ticker.
  • Mobile collapse is clean (two columns become two stacks).

Worse

  • Weekly publish workflow must now write 6 numbers, not 1 (regime + 5 indicators).
  • The chart needs to be regenerated weekly — a small but real ops cost.
  • Risks looking like a dashboard-lite if the evidence panel grows past five rows.
  • "Refreshed Tue 09:41 GMT" implies more liveness than the homepage actually has.
Marketing-page risk
LowEverything we add is a verifiable fact — a number, a date, a count of weeks. None of it is a claim about the product; all of it is the product.
Effort
Medium · ~2 daysBuild is straightforward (a second column, a small SVG chart). The real lift is the publish-time workflow that writes 6 values into the page each Tuesday.

Real writing above the fold
Three to four paragraphs of editorial type fill the first viewport with substance. There's no "feels basic" question when 200 words of actual research are sitting in front of the reader.
Margin call-out
A literal margin note holding the regime, set apart from the prose by a thin rule and a different typographic vocabulary (mono). Visual interest and the cycle state, in one move.
Word count in the topline

Trade-offs vs. current (v4)

Better

  • The strongest possible proof that the product is good: show the product.
  • Lowest build cost — almost no new components, just an article layout.
  • SEO upside: real content on the homepage, weekly refresh.
  • The "why this isn't generic crypto Twitter" question answers itself in three paragraphs.

Worse

  • If a given week's lede is weak, the homepage is weak that week.
  • Writing burden: the first 200 words of every issue now have to be homepage-worthy.
  • Risks duplicating the /issue/47/ permalink — duplicate-content SEO friction.
  • Demoting the cycle state weakens the "what does the indicator mean?" answer.
Marketing-page risk
MediumLowest risk of marketing language but highest risk of marketing theatre — "showing the product" is itself a marketing move. Mitigation: never tweak the opening for the homepage. Whatever Tuesday's lede is, that's what readers see.
Effort
Low · ~1 dayRe-uses existing article styles. Mostly a templating change: pipe the latest issue's first 200 words into the homepage template at publish time.

Visual moves that make it less sparse
The ledger itself
Twenty-four mono rows on the homepage is a lot of substance — and every row is a verifiable fact (a state, a date, a duration). This is the densest possible homepage that's still calm, because the typography is consistent and the structure is a list.
"0 REVISIONS" in the topline
A single token in the masthead strip that no competitor can claim, that proves itself ("if we revised, that token would say something else"), and that says everything about the brand's posture. The whole positioning argument compressed into eight characters.
"Week 7" + "Began Tue 25 Mar."
Two mono lines that give the current call a temporal anchor. The page never says "act now" — it says "this has been true since March." Time becomes the argument.
Hairline-rule left border on the "NOW" block
A 2px orange-gradient rule running the full height of the current entry. Marks it as "the live row" without using a glow, badge, or pulse. Borrows from print typography (the "drop bar" used to mark editorial pull-quotes).
Trade-offs vs. current (v4)

Better

  • Highest brand differentiation of the three. Nobody else does this.
  • Solves "feels basic" by replacing whitespace with public record.
  • Implicit credibility — the ledger proves track record without making any claim.
  • Routes naturally to a new /ledger/ page that becomes a long-term SEO and PR asset.
  • Reframes the entire product: not "a newsletter" but "a public discipline."

Worse

  • Requires you to actually maintain the ledger. The first revised call destroys the value of the token.
  • Demands a "what happens at issue 0" answer — if the site is <6 months old, the ledger is too short to impress.
  • If the current state has held a long time (12+ weeks), the homepage starts to feel static.
  • Newcomers may not understand the ledger until they read the methodology — small first-touch friction.
Marketing-page risk
LowThe page is a list of dates. Marketing language can't survive in a list of dates.
Effort
Medium · ~2 daysTwo-column hero is easy. The lift is (a) building the source-of-truth ledger data file, (b) the new /ledger/ page, (c) the per-issue routing so every row clicks through to its issue.

Recommendation

Ship Direction C (The Ledger), paired with Copy Set 03 (Honest about uncertainty).

The "feels basic" problem in v4 isn't a layout problem; it's a claim problem. The page makes one assertion (here is the current regime) and asks you to trust it. The Ledger replaces that assertion with a record — every regime call ever made, dated, with hold-durations, in mono. The page becomes evidence of itself. That reframes the entire site from "a newsletter with a dashboard" to "a public discipline that publishes weekly" — which is the right positioning, and the one no competitor can copy without doing the work.

Pair it with Set 03 because the ledger and the "honest-about-uncertainty" voice reinforce each other: the page invites you to audit the writer, and the voice paragraph names that fact directly. "I will be wrong sometimes. You'll know when, and how, and against which indicator."
