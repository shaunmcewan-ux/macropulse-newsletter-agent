Design Review

Three ways to open the front door.

The homepage isn't a dashboard, an archive, or a sales page — it's a fifteen-second answer to "what is this, and why should I read it?" Three directions, each making a different bet about how that answer is best told.

For: macropulse.uk System: see /Design System.html Effort: low / med / high

The fifteen-second contract

A first-time visitor must leave the first viewport knowing four things: what MacroPulse is (a weekly research note), what they get (one regime call per week), what the cycle indicator means (four states, one of them currently lit), and why this isn't generic crypto Twitter (the methodology, written down).

What every direction must keep

The cycle state — BTC Accumulation as of this writing — must appear as the page's loudest word, not just an icon. The four-state rail appears in some form on every direction. Orange used once per viewport. Static HTML, mobile-first. The homepage never embeds the live tape.

01

The Editorial Front Door

Treat the homepage like the cover of a research weekly: one display headline, one italic lede, the four cycle states as a static rail beneath, and a single primary CTA. Below the fold, three sample issues and the methodology in one paragraph.

The four states appear on first paint, not after a scroll. The active state is rendered in italic Fraunces, not just a pill — so "BTC Accumulation" reads as a word, the page's thesis. The lede declares both the audience ("allocators who already know the news") and the cadence in one sentence.

Section-by-section
01 · Hero
Mono eyebrow, display headline, italic lede, four-state rail, single primary CTA, mono fine-print. No screenshots, no second CTA, no testimonials. ~640px tall on desktop.
02 · This week
A horizontal "issue card" for the most recent newsletter — date, regime tag, headline, opening paragraph (~50 words), "Read the full issue →" ghost link. One card, full-width, 1px line border. Establishes what the product actually looks like.
03 · The methodology, in one breath
Two-column block. Left: section-header ("THE METHOD" + "Five indicators. One regime. Written down."). Right: 17px Fraunces paragraph naming the five indicators and the four states, ending with a ghost link to /methodology/.
04 · Sample issues
Three Card components in a 3-column grid (1-col mobile). Each card: dateline · regime tag · headline · 30-word excerpt · "Read sample →". Different regimes across the three cards, so the reader sees the framework adapting.
05 · For whom
A short editorial paragraph addressed directly: "Written for allocators, family offices, and anyone who treats crypto as one asset class among several." No icons. No "perfect for…" lists.
06 · CTA strip
The system's CTA-strip component. "Start with the newsletter." italic kicker, primary button. Once.

How the cycle state pulls in

Reference, not embed. The four-state rail is rendered statically in HTML; the active state ("BTC Accumulation") is inlined at publish time. Each state label is a link to /dashboard/#state-{slug}, and the whole rail's caption is "Live on the dashboard →". No JS, no live data on the homepage — the dashboard owns liveness.

Trade-offs vs. current

Better

  • The fifteen-second contract is met on first paint, on a phone.
  • The cycle state is the loudest word — its job — without a live widget.
  • Cheapest direction to build. Pure HTML/CSS. No JS.
  • Reads like the newsletter, which is the voice you've been investing in.

Worse

  • Quietest of the three — relies on copy quality to land.
  • Static rail can feel "just text" if the visitor doesn't know to look at the dashboard.
  • Less differentiated visually from other indie research sites.

How the cycle state pulls in

Embed at publish time, not at runtime. The current state and a single ISO date are written into the HTML by your weekly publish step (the same step that publishes the newsletter). No iframe, no JS, no CORS — just an editor field that the static page reads. Treats the cycle state as editorial content, the same as a masthead date. The link "Live on the dashboard →" routes anyone who wants real-time data to the right place.

Trade-offs vs. current

Better

  • The thesis word is the largest object on the page — strongest fifteen-second answer.
  • The four-state explainer below the fold solves "what does the cycle indicator mean?" without a separate page.
  • Visually distinctive — no other research site opens with a cycle window.
  • Reinforces the dashboard's existence without duplicating it.

Worse

  • Requires the publish workflow to write state into the homepage weekly.
  • Risk of looking like a dashboard-lite if the window isn't restrained.
  • If the cycle state doesn't change for months, the homepage looks frozen.
03

The Letter

Section-by-section
01 · The letter
Mono dateline, four-paragraph letter in 19px Fraunces, italic sign-off, single CTA. Indented like a letter — left margin ~40px on desktop. ~140 words total.
02 · The framework, drawn
Below the letter, a single horizontal block: the four-state rail at full width, with each state's name in Fraunces 22px and a one-sentence definition in 14.5px ink-2 underneath. The page's didactic core. No card chassis — just rules above and below.
03 · From the archive
Three Card components, but treated as a "from the archive" section — older issues across different regimes, framed as "what the framework looked like in March / February / January". Demonstrates consistency over time.
04 · A note on what this is not
Italic Fraunces paragraph (no eyebrow), 17px, set centred at 62ch: "MacroPulse is research and education. Not investment advice. Not a signals service. Not a community. One letter, every Tuesday." Acts as the disclaimer + differentiation in one breath.
05 · Sign-off CTA
Quiet CTA strip — a single italic kicker ("If this is the lens you've been missing, start with Tuesday's letter.") and a primary button. No headline.

How the cycle state pulls in

Inlined in prose. The state lives inside paragraph two of the letter, italicised in the gradient. The dashboard is referenced with "The full case is on the dashboard" as a sentence ending — a link, but worded as continuation, not as navigation. No rail above the fold, no widgets. The letter is the cycle communication.

Trade-offs vs. current

Better

  • Highest brand differentiation. No competitor opens this way.
  • The voice is the product — and visitors meet the voice immediately.
  • "Why this isn't generic crypto Twitter" answers itself; the form is the answer.
  • Cheap to ship. ~80 lines of HTML.

Worse

  • Demands the letter be excellent every week — a writing burden, not just a design choice.
  • Visitors who came expecting "what does the cycle indicator mean?" have to scroll past the letter to learn.
  • Less scannable. A reader who doesn't read the letter sees nothing.
  • Risks looking like a Substack landing page if the type isn't pulled hard.
Recommendation

Ship The Cycle Window, with a sentence borrowed from The Letter.

The fifteen-second contract is the brief. Direction 02 honours it most literally: the cycle state is the largest object on the page, the four-state rail is right there, the editorial framing is one paragraph, and there's exactly one thing to do next. It also reinforces the dashboard without duplicating it — the homepage and dashboard become complementary surfaces of the same idea, which is the right architecture.

Borrow one move from Direction 03: replace the generic editorial paragraph under the cycle window with a two-sentence italic note in the author's voice — the kind of sentence that opens The Letter. That single change answers "why this isn't generic crypto commentary" without a separate "for whom" section, and gives the page a person behind it.

Hold Direction 01 (Editorial Front Door) as the no-build fallback if Direction 02's publish-time embedding turns out to be a bigger lift than expected. The static rail and three sample cards still meet the contract — quieter, but solid.

Direction 03 (The Letter) is the right answer if you decide MacroPulse should compete on voice rather than on framework. It isn't, yet — the cycle indicator is the visible IP and the homepage should foreground it. Revisit if the brand evolves toward author-as-product.
